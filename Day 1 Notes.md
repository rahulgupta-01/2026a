# Classroom Management Strategies Observed

## 1. Signal to Begin

Alex regularly used “3…2…1” as a signal to begin. This helped students understand that they needed to stop talking, focus on the teacher and prepare to listen.

Exact lines Alex used included:

- “3…2…1, go.”
- “3…2…1, eyes to the board, ears listening to me.”
- “3…2…1, eyes on your papers, ears listening to me.”
- “3…2…1, eyes towards me, ears listening to me.”

This routine was used across different year levels and helped create consistency.

## 2. Wait Time and Calm Redirection

Alex used the exact line “Still waiting” when students were not focused. Instead of speaking over students, he paused and waited for attention. This was a calm way to redirect the class without escalating behaviour.

## 3. Planned Ignore

During the Year 7 lesson, a student asked to go to the toilet while another student was already out with the toilet pass. Alex did not respond further and continued teaching.

This was an example of planned ignore. Alex chose not to engage with a repeated or low-level request because responding further could have interrupted the lesson and encouraged more off-task behaviour. Instead, he maintained the classroom routine that only one student could leave at a time.

## 4. Behaviour Tracking

Alex wrote some students’ names on the board for toilet requests and also had a separate list of students who had created disruptions during the lesson. This helped him monitor behaviour and manage routines without stopping the lesson repeatedly.

## 5. Clear Classroom Routines

The use of notebook containers, lining up before entering, toilet pass procedures, countdowns and repeated verbal cues created predictable routines for students. These routines supported classroom organisation and reduced unnecessary interruptions.

# Overall Reflection So Far

Today I observed several important classroom routines and behaviour management strategies. Alex consistently used countdowns, verbal cues, visual slides, wait time and clear transitions to support student focus. His use of “3…2…1” as a signal to begin was effective because students heard the same cue repeatedly across different parts of the lesson.

I also observed how Alex used planned ignore to maintain classroom routines. When a student asked to go to the toilet while another student was already out, Alex did not stop the lesson to discuss it. This helped maintain the flow of teaching and reinforced the existing toilet pass procedure.

Another key takeaway was the importance of consistency. Alex used the same phrases across different classes, including “Eyes to the board, ears listening to me,” “Eyes on your papers, ears listening to me,” “Eyes towards me, ears listening to me,” and “Still waiting.” These repeated cues helped students understand expectations and supported a calm classroom environment.

I also noticed the role of Education Assistants in supporting students. In the Year 9 class, one EA moved around the room to help students, while another was present for one-on-one support. The use of a tablet to photograph the screen showed how small adjustments can help students access learning materials.

Overall, the day showed me how classroom management depends on clear routines, calm redirection, consistent teacher language and positive relationships with students.
